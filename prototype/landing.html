Why does every SubScore start at 0.50?

Think of it like meeting a stranger. You don't assume they're dangerous — that would be unfair. You don't assume they're your best friend — that would be naive. You're neutral. Watchful, but not hostile.

0.50 is that feeling, expressed as math. It means "we genuinely don't know." Not bad. Not good. Unknown.

As the agent builds a track record, the score moves. Clean interactions push it toward 1.0. Incidents pull it toward 0.0. The more evidence there is, the more confident the score — and the harder it is for any single event to change it. Just like a person with 20 years of good credit isn't ruined by one late payment.

How It Works

Three verbs. That's the whole product.

01
Register
An agent registers with Submantle and receives a cryptographic token. Starts at 0.50 — the score for "unknown." Like a new credit card holder with no history.
02
Record
Every interaction builds history. Third parties report incidents. Submantle records them — it never detects. Banks report missed payments to the bureau. Same model.
03
Score
A deterministic formula turns records into a SubScore. Businesses check it before trusting an agent with their data. They set their own thresholds. Submantle never decides.

Think of it like a credit score — but for software

That's a SubScore.

When you apply for a mortgage, the bank doesn't trust your word. They check your credit score — a number built from years of financial behavior, reported by third parties, computed by a neutral bureau.

AI agents need the same thing. When an agent asks to access your data, you need to know: has this agent behaved well before? Who says so? How much evidence exists?

Submantle is that bureau. Neutral, deterministic, portable.
